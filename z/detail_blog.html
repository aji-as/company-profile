Beyond the Bean: Our Commitment to Direct Trade in Ethiopia

Elena Rodriguez

Head of Coffee • Oct 24, 2023

The journey of a single coffee cherry from the high-altitude forests of Guji to your morning cup at Kanara House is a story of thousands of miles, hundreds of hands, and one unwavering commitment to equity.

For years, the coffee industry has relied on complex supply chains that often obscure the identity and the struggles of the people at the source. At Kanara House, we decided early on that "business as usual" wasn't enough. We wanted to see the soil, shake the hands of the producers, and understand the true cost of quality.

“

Sustainability isn't just about the environment; it's about sustaining the livelihoods of the families who make our morning ritual possible.

— Elena Rodriguez “

The Guji Highlands

This spring, our team traveled back to the Oromia region of Ethiopia. Reaching the washing stations in Guji requires a three-day journey through winding mountain paths, but the reward is unparalleled. Here, coffee grows under the canopy of ancient indigenous trees, benefiting from nutrient-rich volcanic soil and a climate that allows cherries to ripen slowly, developing complex floral and stone fruit notes.

What makes our Direct Trade model different is transparency. By bypassing traditional auctions, we are able to pay premiums that are significantly higher than Fair Trade minimums. During this visit, we finalized our second year of a long-term partnership with the Kercha cooperative, which has allowed them to invest in a new water filtration system for their processing facility.

Why Precision Matters

Back in Seattle, we treat these beans with the reverence they deserve. Our roasting profile for the Guji Guji Heirloom is delicate. We use a light-medium roast to preserve the jasmine and peach acidity that characterizes this specific lot. Every morning, our baristas calibrate the grinders to ensure that whether you're drinking a pour-over or an oat milk latte, the terroir of Oromia shines through.
